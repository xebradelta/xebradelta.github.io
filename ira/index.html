Assumptions & fine print

How it computes. Growth compounds monthly at the annual rate you set; contributions are spread evenly across the year (frequency mainly changes the annual total: weekly ×52, monthly ×12, quarterly ×4, yearly ×1). “Today's dollars” discounts every future value by your inflation rate.

IRA contribution limit. For 2026 the IRA limit is $7,500 per year. It adjusts upward over time, so a projection that exceeds it in later years may be fine — but this tool warns you when a scenario contributes above it, since projections that assume over-limit contributions can mislead.

Minors need earned income. A young person can only contribute (or have contributed on their behalf) up to what they actually earned that year — allowance doesn't count, but wages, self-employment, and odd-job income generally do.

Custodial accounts hand over. A custodial IRA transfers to the young person's own control at the age of majority — 18 to 21 depending on the state (marked on the chart at 18).

Roth vs Traditional. Roth: contributions are after-tax, qualified withdrawals after 59½ are tax-free — the headline number is what they'd keep. Traditional: contributions may be deductible, withdrawals are taxed as income — the after-tax figure uses the withdrawal tax rate you set, which is only a guess about the far future.

This is an educational estimate, not advice. Real returns vary year to year; taxes, limits, and laws change. Talk to a professional before making decisions.
